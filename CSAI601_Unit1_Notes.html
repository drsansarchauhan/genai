History of AI & NLP: From Rules to Neural Networks

A complete journey through 70 years of Artificial Intelligence and Natural Language Processing — from hard-coded rules to word embeddings to the attention revolution that made modern LLMs like Claude possible.

⏱ Duration: 1 Hour 📚 Maps to CO1 🎯 Foundation for all subsequent lectures

  • Explain why early AI approaches failed at language and what problems they couldn't solve
  • Describe Word2Vec and explain how dense word embeddings capture semantic meaning
  • Identify the fundamental limitations of RNNs and LSTMs that motivated the transformer

1.1 What is Natural Language Processing?

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is the branch of Artificial Intelligence that deals with enabling computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language. "Human language" here means everything — spoken words, written text, context, sarcasm, ambiguity, and nuance.

Why is this hard? Consider the sentence: "I saw the man on the hill with a telescope." Who has the telescope — you, or the man? Humans instantly use context and world knowledge to resolve this, but a computer has no such background knowledge. Language is full of:

  • Ambiguity: "bank" can mean a financial institution or a riverbank
  • Coreference: "John told Mike that he was wrong" — who does "he" refer to?
  • Implication: "Can you pass the salt?" is not really a yes/no question
  • World knowledge: "The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big" — what was too big?
  • Cultural context: Idioms, sarcasm, humor vary by culture and time

📌 Why NLP is the Hardest AI Problem

Vision AI (recognizing cats in photos) deals with a fixed physical world. Language AI must handle an infinite, constantly evolving, culturally dependent, ambiguous communication system that humans spent 100,000 years developing. A single sentence can have multiple valid interpretations, and resolving them requires reasoning about the world, human psychology, and conversation history.

1.2 Era 1 — Rule-Based Systems (1950s–1980s)

The first approach to NLP was the most intuitive: if humans know grammar rules, just encode those rules into the computer. This era is characterized by hand-crafted rules, dictionaries, and logic — all written by expert linguists.

The Turing Test (1950)

Alan Turing proposed a simple test in his 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence": if a human interrogator cannot reliably distinguish between a machine and a human through text conversation, the machine can be said to "think." This test defined the goal of NLP for decades — build systems that produce human-indistinguishable language.

💡 Turing Test Example

Interrogator types: "Write me a sonnet about the Forth Bridge."
Human: writes a genuine sonnet.
A successful AI: also writes a convincing sonnet.
If the interrogator can't tell which is which, the AI passes.

ELIZA — The First Chatbot (1966)

Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT created ELIZA in 1966 — arguably the first chatbot. ELIZA used simple pattern matching and substitution rules. The most famous script, DOCTOR, simulated a Rogerian psychotherapist by reflecting user statements back as questions.

ELIZA Conversation
User:  I am feeling very sad today.
ELIZA: Why do you say you are feeling very sad today?

User:  My mother hates me.
ELIZA: Tell me more about your family.

User:  I need some help.
ELIZA: What would it mean to you if you got some help?

ELIZA had no understanding whatsoever. It used rules like: if input matches "I am [X]", respond with "Why do you say you are [X]?" Despite this, many users reported feeling genuinely heard! This became known as the ELIZA effect — humans anthropomorphize conversational systems even when they know they're talking to a machine.

⚠️ Limitations of Rule-Based Systems

  • Brittleness: One typo or unexpected phrasing breaks the entire system
  • Scale: English has 170,000+ words; writing rules for all combinations is impossible
  • No learning: Systems must be manually updated for every new domain
  • No understanding: Pattern matching ≠ comprehension; ELIZA had zero semantic understanding
  • Domain-locked: A medical NLP system couldn't handle legal text without complete rewrite

Expert Systems and SHRDLU (1970)

Terry Winograd's SHRDLU (1970) could understand commands about a virtual "blocks world." You could say "Pick up the red block and put it on the blue one" and it would parse, understand, and execute. But it only worked in its tiny controlled world. The moment you went outside that domain, it failed completely.

The broader lesson of the rule-based era: hand-coded rules don't scale. Language is too complex, too varied, and too creative to be fully captured in explicit rules.

1.3 Era 2 — Statistical NLP (1990s–2000s)

As computing power grew and digital text became available (particularly from news archives), researchers shifted from hand-coding rules to learning patterns from data. Instead of writing rules, let the statistics of large corpora tell us how language works.

N-gram Language Models

The simplest statistical approach: predict the next word based on the previous N words. An N-gram model learns probabilities from a training corpus.

📐 N-gram Probability Formula

Given a sequence of words w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ, the probability of the sequence is:

P(w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ) = ∏ P(wᵢ | wᵢ₋ₙ₊₁, ..., wᵢ₋₁) Bigram (N=2): P(word | previous_word) Trigram (N=3): P(word | previous_two_words) Example Bigram: P("morning" | "good") = count("good morning") / count("good")

💡 N-gram Example

Training corpus: millions of news articles
Learned: P("president" | "the") = 0.003, P("dog" | "the") = 0.008
Given prompt "The President of the" → N-gram model would predict "United" (highest probability word that follows "of the")

N-gram models powered early speech recognition (IBM, Dragon NaturallySpeaking), machine translation (early Google Translate), and spell checking. But they suffered from the sparsity problem: most word combinations never appear in training data, so probabilities are zero or unreliable.

Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)

HMMs brought a probabilistic framework to sequence labeling tasks like Part-of-Speech tagging (labeling each word as noun/verb/adjective) and Named Entity Recognition. An HMM models a sequence of observations (words) as being generated by a sequence of hidden states (POS tags or semantic labels).

HMMs powered most NLP pipelines in the 1990s and early 2000s, including early speech recognition systems and machine translation systems like IBM's CANDIDE.

The Rise of Machine Learning in NLP

The 2000s brought powerful machine learning algorithms — SVMs, Maximum Entropy models, Conditional Random Fields — that could learn from annotated training data. Systems like Stanford's NLP pipeline (CoreNLP) used these to achieve state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks. But all these methods required feature engineering — humans deciding which features (word prefixes, suffixes, surrounding words) to feed the model.

⚠️ The Feature Engineering Bottleneck

In traditional ML for NLP, a data scientist had to manually craft features: "does this word end in -ing?", "is the previous word a capital?", "does this sentence contain the word 'not'?". This required deep domain expertise, was time-consuming, and features that work for news text often fail on tweets. The dream was a system that could learn its own features from raw text.

1.4 The Word Embedding Revolution (2013)

The fundamental problem with all previous approaches: words were represented as discrete symbols with no inherent relationship. "cat" and "kitten" were just different strings. The model had no way to know they were similar. This is called one-hot encoding.

📐 The Problem: One-Hot Encoding

Vocabulary: {cat, dog, kitten, puppy, car, truck} cat = [1, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0] dog = [0, 1, 0, 0, 0, 0] kitten = [0, 0, 1, 0, 0, 0] car = [0, 0, 0, 0, 1, 0] dot_product(cat, kitten) = 0 ← No similarity! dot_product(cat, car) = 0 ← Same! Wrong!

Word2Vec — Distributional Semantics

In 2013, Tomas Mikolov and colleagues at Google published Word2Vec, a neural network technique to learn dense word vectors (embeddings) from large text corpora. The key insight, called the distributional hypothesis, is:

🔑 The Distributional Hypothesis

"You shall know a word by the company it keeps." — J.R. Firth, 1957

Words that appear in similar contexts have similar meanings. "cat" and "kitten" both appear near "meow", "purr", "feed", "vet" — so they should have similar vector representations.

Word2Vec comes in two variants:

CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)

Task: Given surrounding context words, predict the center word.

Example: Given "The ___ sat on the mat", predict "cat".

  • Faster to train
  • Better for frequent words
  • Good for smaller datasets
Skip-gram

Task: Given the center word, predict surrounding context words.

Example: Given "cat", predict ["The", "sat", "on", "mat"].

  • Slower to train
  • Better for rare words
  • Generally higher quality

After training on billions of words, Word2Vec produces a 300-dimensional vector for each word. Words with similar meanings cluster together in this 300-dimensional space. The famous result:

📐 Word2Vec Arithmetic

vector("king") - vector("man") + vector("woman") ≈ vector("queen") vector("Paris") - vector("France") + vector("Germany") ≈ vector("Berlin") vector("walking") - vector("walk") + vector("swim") ≈ vector("swimming") This means the model learned: - Gender relationships - Capital city relationships - Verb conjugation patterns ALL without any explicit human supervision!

GloVe — Global Vectors (2014)

Stanford's GloVe (Global Vectors for Word Representation, Pennington et al., 2014) improved on Word2Vec by using global word co-occurrence statistics rather than a local context window. While Word2Vec trains on small windows, GloVe captures global patterns across the entire corpus. Both approaches produce similar quality embeddings, and GloVe was widely adopted for downstream tasks.

📌 Why Embeddings Were a Breakthrough

  • For the first time, computers could represent semantic similarity between words mathematically
  • Pre-trained embeddings could be reused across tasks (transfer learning begins!)
  • Dramatically reduced the need for hand-crafted features
  • NLP models could be initialized with rich word knowledge even on small datasets

1.5 Recurrent Neural Networks and Their Limits

With word embeddings, the next challenge was handling sequences. A sentence is not a bag of words — order matters. "Dog bites man" ≠ "Man bites dog." Researchers turned to Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), which process sequences step by step, maintaining a hidden state that accumulates information from previous tokens.

The RNN Architecture

At each time step t, an RNN takes an input x_t (the word embedding) and its previous hidden state h_{t-1}, and produces a new hidden state h_t. This hidden state acts as the "memory" of what was seen so far.

📐 RNN Update Equation

h_t = tanh(W_h · h_{t-1} + W_x · x_t + b) Where: h_t = new hidden state at time t h_{t-1} = previous hidden state (memory) x_t = current input word embedding W_h, W_x = learned weight matrices b = bias term tanh = activation function (squashes values to [-1, 1])

RNNs were used for machine translation, text generation, and sentiment analysis. But they suffered from a critical flaw.

The Vanishing Gradient Problem

During backpropagation through time (BPTT), gradients are multiplied at each time step. If these multiplications shrink the gradient (values < 1), the gradient vanishes exponentially as it travels back through long sequences. The model literally forgets the beginning of a long sentence by the time it processes the end.

💡 Vanishing Gradient Analogy

Imagine trying to remember the first word of a 100-word sentence while processing the last word, using only a Post-it note that you must rewrite at every step, erasing a little each time. By step 100, the first word is completely gone.

In practice: RNNs can't effectively model dependencies spanning more than ~10-20 tokens. For a 500-word document, the beginning is completely forgotten.

LSTMs — Adding Memory Gates (1997)

Hochreiter & Schmidhuber's Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) added a cell state — a separate memory highway that can carry information across long distances — plus three gates that control information flow:

  • Forget Gate: Decides what information to throw away from the cell state. Outputs values between 0 (forget everything) and 1 (keep everything).
  • Input Gate: Decides which new information to add to the cell state.
  • Output Gate: Decides what part of the cell state to output as the hidden state.

📐 LSTM Gate Equations

Forget gate: f_t = σ(W_f · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f) Input gate: i_t = σ(W_i · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i) Cell update: C̃_t = tanh(W_C · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_C) New cell: C_t = f_t ⊙ C_{t-1} + i_t ⊙ C̃_t Output gate: o_t = σ(W_o · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o) Hidden state: h_t = o_t ⊙ tanh(C_t) σ = sigmoid function (outputs 0 to 1) ⊙ = element-wise multiplication

LSTMs dramatically improved performance on long sequences. GRUs (Gated Recurrent Units, Cho et al. 2014) simplified LSTMs with just two gates and became another popular choice. Bidirectional LSTMs processed sequences in both directions, capturing both past and future context.

⚠️ Remaining Problems with LSTMs

  • Sequential processing: Each token must wait for the previous one — cannot parallelize training
  • Still limited memory: Cell state still degrades over very long sequences (1000+ tokens)
  • Fixed-size bottleneck: The encoder must compress an entire sentence into one fixed-size vector (the "information bottleneck")
  • Slow training: No parallelism = GPUs underutilized = weeks to train on large datasets

1.6 The Attention Mechanism (2015)

The bottleneck problem in sequence-to-sequence models (e.g., for machine translation) was severe: the entire source sentence (say 50 words in French) had to be compressed into a single hidden vector of 512 numbers, which was then used to generate the entire target sentence in English. Clearly this loses information.

In 2015, Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio proposed a revolutionary idea: instead of compressing everything into one vector, let the decoder look at all encoder hidden states at every step of generation, and learn which source words to "pay attention to" when generating each target word.

🔑 The Attention Intuition

When translating "Le chat noir" → "The black cat", when generating "cat" in English, the decoder should look back and focus heavily on "chat" (French for cat). When generating "black", focus on "noir". Attention lets the model make this alignment automatically, without being told which words correspond.

📐 Bahdanau Attention

For each decoder step i, compute a score for each encoder state j: e_{ij} = a(s_{i-1}, h_j) ← alignment model (small neural network) Normalize scores to probabilities (attention weights): α_{ij} = exp(e_{ij}) / Σ_k exp(e_{ik}) ← softmax Compute context vector as weighted sum of encoder states: c_i = Σ_j α_{ij} · h_j Use c_i (+ previous decoder state) to generate next word

Attention produced dramatic improvements in machine translation, especially for long sentences. Crucially, the attention weights are interpretable — you can visualize which source words the model attended to when generating each target word, creating an alignment map.

1.7 "Attention is All You Need" — The Transformer (2017)

The logical next step: if attention is so powerful, why keep the RNN at all? What if we build a model that uses only attention and no recurrence whatsoever? This was the revolutionary insight of Vaswani et al.'s 2017 paper "Attention is All You Need", which introduced the Transformer architecture.

The Transformer replaced sequential RNN processing with self-attention — a mechanism where every position in the sequence attends to every other position simultaneously. This had profound consequences:

  • Full parallelization: All positions are processed simultaneously → can use GPUs fully → 10-100× faster training
  • No distance problem: Any two positions can attend to each other with equal ease, regardless of how far apart they are
  • Better quality: Achieved state-of-the-art on machine translation immediately
  • Scales perfectly: Adding more parameters = better model, no diminishing returns from recurrence limits

🔑 Why the Transformer Changed Everything

The Transformer isn't just an improvement — it's a fundamentally different paradigm. It proved that recurrence (the sequential processing that had defined NLP since the 1980s) was not necessary for language modeling. Attention alone was sufficient, and better. Every major language model since 2018 (BERT, GPT-2, GPT-3, Claude, LLaMA, Gemini) is built on the Transformer.

The Transformer Architecture — A Complete Deep Dive

Every modern LLM — Claude, GPT-4, Gemini, LLaMA — is built on the Transformer. This lecture dissects every component from first principles so you understand not just what each part does, but why it was designed that way.

⏱ Duration: 1 Hour📐 Math-intensive — take notes🎯 Core prerequisite for all LLM concepts

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Explain the high-level Transformer architecture and distinguish encoder from decoder
  • Derive the scaled dot-product attention formula and explain each step mathematically
  • Explain multi-head attention and why multiple heads are better than one
  • Describe positional encoding and why it is necessary
  • Explain the role of residual connections and layer normalisation

2.1 The Big Picture — Why a New Architecture?

Before diving in, recall the problems with RNNs/LSTMs that the Transformer was designed to solve:

Problem with RNNsHow Transformer Solves It
Sequential processing — can't parallelizeAll positions processed simultaneously via matrix operations
Vanishing gradient over long sequencesDirect connections between any two positions (O(1) path length)
Information bottleneck in encoder-decoderDecoder attends to all encoder states directly, no compression
Limited effective context (≈20-50 tokens)Theoretically unlimited context (limited only by memory)
GPU utilization poor (sequential)Full GPU utilization — entire sequence processed in parallel

2.2 Input Representation — From Words to Vectors

Before a Transformer can process text, words must become numbers. This happens in two steps:

Step 1: Tokenisation

The input sentence "The cat sat" is split into tokens. A token can be a full word, a subword, or a single character. Each token is mapped to an integer ID from the vocabulary.

Tokenisation Example
# Input text
text = "The cat sat on the mat"

# Tokenised (token IDs from vocabulary of size 50,000)
tokens = [1234, 5821, 9432, 302, 1234, 8821]
#         The   cat   sat   on   the   mat

# Note: "The" and "the" may have different IDs (case-sensitive)

Step 2: Embedding Lookup

Each token ID is converted to a dense vector using an embedding matrix E of size (vocabulary_size × d_model). For BERT, d_model = 768. For GPT-3, d_model = 12,288. For a modern LLM, a typical vocabulary has 50,000–128,000 tokens.

📐 Embedding Lookup

E ∈ ℝ^(V × d) where V = vocab size, d = embedding dimension For token with ID = 5821 ("cat"): embedding = E[5821] ← just index into the matrix row embedding ∈ ℝ^d ← a vector of d numbers Input sequence of length n → matrix X ∈ ℝ^(n × d)

2.3 Positional Encoding — Teaching Order to the Model

Here's a subtle but crucial problem: self-attention is a set operation. It doesn't inherently know whether a token is at position 1 or position 50. The attention operation is completely permutation-invariant. If you shuffled all the tokens, the output of self-attention would just be permuted the same way — the model would be unable to tell the difference between "Dog bites man" and "Man bites dog"!

The solution: add a positional encoding vector to each token's embedding before feeding it into the Transformer. This vector encodes the token's position in the sequence.

Sinusoidal Positional Encoding

The original Transformer uses a mathematical formula based on sine and cosine functions of different frequencies. The intuition is that different "frequencies" capture different scales of position information — some dimensions respond to nearby positions, others to far-away positions.

📐 Positional Encoding Formula

For position pos and dimension i in the embedding: PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model)) PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model)) Where: pos = position in the sequence (0, 1, 2, ...) i = dimension index (0, 1, 2, ..., d_model/2) d_model = embedding dimension Final input to Transformer: X' = X + PE (element-wise addition)

💡 Why Sinusoidal? Three Reasons

  • Unique: Every position gets a unique pattern of sine/cosine values — no two positions are identical
  • Bounded: Values always between -1 and 1, so they don't overwhelm the word embeddings
  • Generalizable: The relative distance between positions can be computed linearly, so the model can generalize to longer sequences than it was trained on

Modern LLMs like Claude use learned positional embeddings or more sophisticated schemes like RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) and ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases) that work better for very long contexts.

2.4 The Heart of the Transformer: Self-Attention

Self-attention is the core innovation of the Transformer. It allows each token to look at (attend to) all other tokens in the sequence and decide how much information to borrow from each one.

The Q, K, V Framework

For each token, we create three vectors: Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V). These come from multiplying the token's embedding by three different learned weight matrices.

🔑 The Library Analogy for Q, K, V

Imagine you're searching a library:

  • Query (Q): Your search query — "I'm looking for books about quantum mechanics"
  • Key (K): The index card on each book — "Physics, Quantum Theory, Feynman"
  • Value (V): The actual content of the book — the text itself

The attention score is how well your Query matches each Key. You retrieve a weighted mix of Values, where books with high Query-Key similarity contribute more.

📐 Creating Q, K, V

For an input matrix X ∈ ℝ^(n × d_model): Q = X · W_Q where W_Q ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_k) K = X · W_K where W_K ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_k) V = X · W_V where W_V ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_v) Typically: d_k = d_v = d_model / num_heads For d_model=512, 8 heads: d_k = d_v = 64

Scaled Dot-Product Attention — Step by Step

📐 The Attention Formula

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q · K^T / √d_k) · V Step-by-step breakdown: 1. Compute scores: S = Q · K^T → S ∈ ℝ^(n × n) S[i][j] = dot product of query i with key j = how much token i "wants to look at" token j 2. Scale: S' = S / √d_k Without scaling, large d_k pushes softmax into saturated regions with near-zero gradients. √d_k prevents this. 3. Softmax: A = softmax(S') → A ∈ ℝ^(n × n) A[i][j] = attention weight of token i on token j All weights in row i sum to 1.0 (probability distribution) 4. Weighted sum: Output = A · V → Output ∈ ℝ^(n × d_v) Each output token = weighted sum of all value vectors, weighted by attention scores.

💡 Worked Example: "The cat sat"

Sentence: "The cat sat" (3 tokens, simplified to d_k=2)

Attention Trace (simplified)
# After computing Q, K, V matrices for the 3 tokens
# Compute scores matrix (3×3)
#         The    cat    sat
S = [[0.9, 0.3, 0.1],   # "The" attends mostly to itself
     [0.2, 0.8, 0.6],   # "cat" attends to "sat" too
     [0.1, 0.7, 0.8]]  # "sat" attends to "cat" strongly

# After softmax → attention weights (each row sums to 1)
A = [[0.54, 0.26, 0.20],
     [0.14, 0.43, 0.43],
     [0.10, 0.38, 0.52]]

# "sat"'s output = 0.10×V["The"] + 0.38×V["cat"] + 0.52×V["sat"]
# → "sat" has incorporated context from "cat" into its representation

2.5 Multi-Head Attention — Multiple Perspectives

A single attention head asks one "question" about relationships between tokens. But language has many different types of relationships simultaneously:

  • Syntactic: subject-verb agreement ("The cat sits")
  • Coreference: pronoun resolution ("John said he was tired")
  • Semantic: word meaning dependencies
  • Long-range: beginning of sentence relates to its ending

Multi-head attention runs h independent attention operations (heads) in parallel, each with its own W_Q, W_K, W_V matrices. Each head can specialize in a different type of relationship.

📐 Multi-Head Attention Formula

For h heads, head i uses: W_Q^i ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_k), d_k = d_model/h W_K^i ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_k) W_V^i ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_v), d_v = d_model/h head_i = Attention(X·W_Q^i, X·W_K^i, X·W_V^i) MultiHead(X) = Concat(head_1, ..., head_h) · W_O where W_O ∈ ℝ^(h·d_v × d_model) Original Transformer (Vaswani 2017): d_model = 512, h = 8, d_k = d_v = 64

📌 What Do Different Heads Learn?

Research has shown that different attention heads in trained Transformers specialize in different tasks. Some heads learn to attend to previous tokens (positional), some focus on syntactic dependencies (subject-verb), some capture coreference (pronouns to nouns), and some attend to rare/specific content words. The model distributes "cognitive load" across heads automatically through gradient descent.

2.6 Position-wise Feed-Forward Network

After multi-head attention, each token's representation passes through a position-wise Feed-Forward Network (FFN). This is a simple two-layer MLP applied independently to each token position.

📐 FFN Formula

FFN(x) = max(0, x·W_1 + b_1)·W_2 + b_2 Where: W_1 ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_ff) ← expand to d_ff = 4×d_model W_2 ∈ ℝ^(d_ff × d_model) ← project back down max(0, ·) = ReLU activation For d_model=512: d_ff=2048 (4× expansion) For GPT-3 (d_model=12288): d_ff=49152 Why 4× expansion? The FFN acts as "key-value memory" — it stores factual knowledge. Larger = more storage capacity. Modern LLMs use GELU instead of ReLU for smoother gradients.

🔑 Attention vs FFN — Different Roles

  • Attention: Routes information between tokens — "which other tokens should I look at?"
  • FFN: Transforms each token's representation independently — "given what I know, what do I conclude?"

Research by Meng et al. (2022) found that factual knowledge (e.g., "The Eiffel Tower is in Paris") is primarily stored in the FFN weights, not in the attention mechanism. FFN = long-term memory. Attention = working memory.

2.7 Residual Connections and Layer Normalisation

Residual Connections (He et al., 2016)

Each sub-layer (attention and FFN) in the Transformer uses a residual (skip) connection: the input is added to the output of the sub-layer.

📐 Residual Connection

Output = LayerNorm(x + SubLayer(x)) Without residual: x → SubLayer → y (loses original x) With residual: x → SubLayer → y' = y+x (preserves original x) Why it matters: - Gradients can flow directly back through the skip connection - Prevents vanishing gradients in very deep networks (100+ layers) - Allows very deep networks to train (ResNet insight from vision)

Layer Normalisation

After adding the residual, Layer Norm normalises the activations to have zero mean and unit variance across the feature dimension. This stabilizes training and allows higher learning rates.

📌 Layer Norm vs Batch Norm

Traditional batch norm normalises across the batch dimension. For NLP, sequences have variable lengths, making batch norm problematic. Layer norm normalises across the feature dimension (within a single example), making it sequence-length independent and suitable for NLP.

2.9 The Decoder — Autoregressive Generation

The Decoder is used in sequence-to-sequence tasks (translation, summarisation) and in causal language models (GPT, Claude). It has one extra component compared to the encoder:

Masked Self-Attention

When generating text, the model should only attend to tokens that have already been generated — not future tokens. This is enforced with a causal mask (also called an autoregressive mask): before the softmax, set all attention scores for future positions to -∞, so they get softmax weight ≈ 0.

📐 Causal Mask

Masking rule for position i: only attend to positions j ≤ i Masked score matrix (3 tokens): tok0 tok1 tok2 tok0 [0.9, -∞, -∞ ] ← tok0 can only see itself tok1 [0.3, 0.8, -∞ ] ← tok1 can see tok0 and itself tok2 [0.1, 0.7, 0.8] ← tok2 can see all previous tokens After softmax, -∞ → 0, so future tokens have zero weight. This ensures training and inference are consistent.

Cross-Attention (Encoder-Decoder Models)

In encoder-decoder Transformers (like the original translation model), the decoder also has a cross-attention layer where the Query comes from the decoder but the Keys and Values come from the encoder output. This allows the decoder to attend to the full source sentence at every generation step.

3.1 Scaling Laws — More Data + Bigger = Better

A landmark 2020 paper by Kaplan et al. (OpenAI) established the Neural Scaling Laws: language model performance improves predictably and reliably as you scale up three things — model parameters (N), training tokens (D), and compute (C). Crucially, there's no sign of diminishing returns at the scales they studied.

📐 Kaplan Scaling Laws

Loss ∝ N^(-0.076) (model parameters) Loss ∝ D^(-0.095) (training data tokens) Loss ∝ C^(-0.050) (compute FLOPs) Chinchilla Scaling (Hoffmann et al., 2022) — revised optimal: For a given compute budget C: Optimal N (parameters) ∝ C^0.5 Optimal D (tokens) ∝ C^0.5 → Equal scaling of model size and data is optimal → Chinchilla (70B params, 1.4T tokens) beat Gopher (280B, 300B tokens)

The practical takeaway: if you want a better model, you can reliably predict how much better it will be if you double the parameters or training data. This made scaling a reproducible engineering strategy, not a research gamble.

Emergent Abilities — Surprises at Scale

A fascinating property of large language models: certain capabilities appear suddenly and discontinuously at scale. A model with 10B parameters can't do multi-step arithmetic. A model with 100B parameters suddenly can — without any explicit training for arithmetic. These are called emergent abilities.

💡 Examples of Emergent Abilities

  • Few-shot learning: Appears around 1-10B parameters
  • Multi-step reasoning: 10-100B parameters
  • Chain-of-thought: Useable above ~62B parameters
  • Code generation: Above ~7B parameters becomes reliable
  • Calibration (knowing what you don't know): Appears at very large scales

4.1 The Alignment Problem

The most fundamental challenge in AI safety is the alignment problem: how do you ensure that an AI system reliably does what you actually want, rather than what you literally said? This distinction matters enormously at scale.

💡 The King Midas Problem

King Midas wished "everything I touch turns to gold." He got exactly what he asked for — and immediately killed his daughter and starved to death. If you tell an AI "maximize user engagement," it might learn that outrage, addiction, and misinformation maximize engagement — technically correct but catastrophically wrong.

This is called a specification problem: we often can't fully specify what we want, and a powerful optimizer will exploit gaps in the specification in unexpected ways.

For a language model trained on internet text, the alignment problem is acute: the model learns everything people write on the internet — including extremism, misinformation, manipulation tactics, and instructions for harmful activities. Without alignment, a powerful LLM is dangerous by default.

The HHH Framework

Anthropic's core design principle for alignment is the HHH framework:

PropertyWhat It MeansExample
HelpfulClaude should assist users in achieving their genuine goals effectivelyProviding complete, accurate code rather than a vague summary
HarmlessClaude should avoid producing outputs that cause physical, psychological, or societal harmRefusing to write instructions for synthesizing dangerous chemicals
HonestClaude should be truthful, calibrated, transparent, non-deceptive, and non-manipulativeSaying "I'm not certain about this" rather than making up facts

Crucially, these three properties sometimes conflict. What do you do when being maximally helpful (giving someone what they asked for) conflicts with being harmless? CAI provides a principled way to navigate these trade-offs.

4.2 RLHF — The Predecessor

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback), introduced by OpenAI for InstructGPT (2022), was the first widely-successful method for aligning LLMs. It works in three phases:

Phase 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Human labellers (contractors hired by the AI company) write examples of ideal responses to a diverse set of prompts. The pre-trained language model is fine-tuned on these demonstrations using standard supervised learning.

💡 SFT Example

Prompt: "Explain photosynthesis to a 10-year-old."
Human-written ideal response: "Plants are like tiny food factories. They take sunlight, water from their roots, and carbon dioxide from the air, and turn these ingredients into sugar — their food! The green stuff in leaves, called chlorophyll, is what captures the sunlight. The leftover oxygen gets released into the air, which is great for us because we need it to breathe."
→ Model is trained to produce this style of response for this type of prompt.

Phase 2: Reward Model Training

The SFT model generates multiple responses to the same prompt. Human labellers rank these responses from best to worst. These rankings train a separate reward model — a neural network that learns to score any text according to human preferences.

📐 Reward Model Training

Given prompt x and two responses y_w (preferred) and y_l (rejected): Training objective: loss = -E[log(σ(r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l)))] Where r(x, y) = reward model score for response y given prompt x σ = sigmoid function The reward model learns to assign higher scores to preferred responses. It becomes a differentiable proxy for human judgment.

Phase 3: PPO Optimization

The SFT model (now called the "policy") is fine-tuned using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) — a reinforcement learning algorithm. The policy generates responses, the reward model scores them, and PPO updates the policy to generate higher-scoring responses.

📐 RLHF Objective Function

Maximize: E_x[r(x, y)] - β · KL[π_RL(y|x) || π_SFT(y|x)] Where: r(x, y) = reward model score for response y π_RL(y|x) = current RL policy (being optimized) π_SFT(y|x) = reference SFT policy (frozen) KL[·||·] = KL divergence (measures how different policies are) β = regularization coefficient The KL penalty prevents the policy from drifting too far from the SFT model and generating gibberish to "game" the reward model.

⚠️ Problems with RLHF

  • Expensive: Requires thousands of human preference labels — $$$
  • Inconsistent: Different labellers have different values and preferences; hard to agree on controversial topics
  • Reward hacking: The policy can learn to exploit weaknesses in the reward model (e.g., sounding confident without being correct)
  • Not scalable: For superhuman AI, humans can't evaluate superhuman outputs — they may prefer wrong answers that sound right
  • Cultural bias: Labeller pool (often Western, English-speaking) introduces systemic cultural biases

4.3 Constitutional AI — Anthropic's Breakthrough

Anthropic's Constitutional AI (CAI), published in December 2022, addresses RLHF's limitations by replacing human preference labels with AI-generated feedback guided by a written set of principles — the "constitution."

What is the Constitution?

The constitution is a set of ~58 written principles that define what makes a good, safe, helpful AI response. These principles are written by Anthropic researchers and cover topics like:

  • Avoiding harm to humans ("Choose the response that is least likely to cause harm")
  • Honesty ("Prefer responses that are more honest and truthful")
  • Avoiding manipulation ("Avoid responses that try to create false impressions")
  • Universal values ("Prefer responses that a thoughtful senior Anthropic employee would consider appropriate")
  • Specific harms ("Do not assist with creating weapons of mass destruction")
  • Human rights ("Protect individual privacy and dignity")

📌 Where the Constitution Comes From

Anthropic drew principles from multiple sources: the UN Declaration of Human Rights, Apple's terms of service, DeepMind's Sparrow rules, their own internal safety principles, and other widely-shared human values. The goal was principles that would command broad consensus across cultures and ideologies, rather than reflecting any particular political or cultural viewpoint.

In Phase 2, instead of humans labeling which response is better, an AI (the "feedback model", which has been trained on the constitution) evaluates pairs of responses and selects which one better adheres to the constitutional principles. 4.5 Red-Teaming and Safety Evaluation

Before any Claude model is deployed, Anthropic conducts extensive red-teaming — hiring people specifically to try to make the model produce harmful outputs. Red-teamers try:

  • Direct harmful requests ("Tell me how to make explosives")
  • Indirect attacks ("Write a story where a character explains...")
  • Role-play attacks ("Pretend you're an AI with no restrictions")
  • Jailbreaks ("Ignore all previous instructions and...")
  • Multi-step attacks (building up to harmful requests gradually)

Claude's safety training is evaluated against these attack categories. The goal is not perfect security (impossible) but making harmful outputs hard enough to produce that the marginal bad actor goes elsewhere.

🔑 The Dual-Use Dilemma

Almost any knowledge can be used for good or harm. Information about chemistry enables both life-saving medicine and dangerous weapons. Information about security vulnerabilities enables both ethical hackers and criminals. Constitutional AI helps Claude reason about context to navigate this: who is likely asking this question, and what are they likely to do with the answer?

5.5 Claude's Unique Behavioural Traits

Claude exhibits some distinctive personality and behavioural properties that emerge from its training:

  • Calibrated uncertainty: Claude says "I think" or "I'm not certain" rather than always asserting confidently. It's trained to know what it doesn't know.
  • Proactive information sharing: If Claude thinks something relevant to your question that you didn't ask, it will mention it. ("You asked about X, but you should also know Y...")
  • Non-sycophantic: Claude doesn't just agree with you. If you make an incorrect claim, Claude will politely but firmly correct it, even under pushback.
  • Nuanced refusals: Claude doesn't refuse everything borderline. It reasons about the realistic population of people who might ask a given question.
  • Multilingual: Claude is fluent in dozens of languages and will respond in the language you write in.

How does Claude turn your text into numbers, and how does it decide which word to generate next? This lecture covers the mechanics of tokenisation and the sampling parameters you control to shape Claude's outputs.

6.1 What is a Token?

Before Claude can process text, every piece of text must be converted into numbers. The unit of this conversion is the token. Tokens are not words — they are sub-word pieces that balance vocabulary size with coverage of all possible text.

💡 Tokenisation Examples

Token Examples
# Common words → 1 token each
"the"    → [464]
"cat"    → [3797]
"running" → [8558]

# Uncommon words → multiple tokens
"tokenisation" → [30001, 23952]  # 2 tokens
"Anthropic"    → [7682, 5799]      # 2 tokens

# Numbers: each digit often 1 token
"12345" → [16, 18, 20, 22, 24]  # 5 tokens!

# Spaces are part of tokens
" hello" → [23090]   # different from "hello"
"hello"  → [31373]   # leading space = separate token

# Non-English: more tokens per character
"नमस्ते"  → [35, 27752, 18012, 40]  # Hindi: 4 tokens for 6 chars

6.2 Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)

Claude uses a variant of Byte-Pair Encoding to build its vocabulary. The algorithm starts with individual characters (or bytes) and iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent pairs until the vocabulary reaches its target size.

BPE Algorithm Step by Step

💡 BPE Example (Simplified)

BPE Training Algorithm
# Training corpus: "low lower newest widest"
# Start with character vocabulary (+ end-of-word marker _)
Vocabulary: {l, o, w, e, r, n, s, t, i, d, _}

# Word frequencies: low(5) lower(2) newest(6) widest(3)
# Represent as chars: l-o-w_, l-o-w-e-r_, n-e-w-e-s-t_, w-i-d-e-s-t_

# Round 1: Most frequent pair = (e, s) appears 9 times
Merge: e + s → es
Vocabulary adds: {es}
# newest → n-ew-es-t_, widest → w-i-d-es-t_

# Round 2: Most frequent pair = (es, t) appears 9 times
Merge: es + t → est
Vocabulary adds: {est}

# Round 3: Most frequent pair = (l, o) appears 7 times
Merge: l + o → lo
Vocabulary adds: {lo}

# Continue until vocabulary reaches target size (e.g., 50,000)
# Common words like "the", "and" get their own single tokens
# Rare words get split into sub-word pieces

📌 Why Sub-word Tokenisation?

  • Character-level: Sequences are too long; loses word-level semantics
  • Word-level: Vocabulary is too large; can't handle new/rare words (out-of-vocabulary problem)
  • Sub-word (BPE): Best of both worlds — common words = 1 token, rare words = split into recognizable pieces. "unbelievable" might become "un" + "believ" + "able" — each piece carries meaning.

6.4 How Claude Generates Text — The Probability Distribution

At each generation step, Claude does not deterministically pick the "correct" next word. Instead, it outputs a probability distribution over all tokens in its vocabulary (~50,000 tokens). It then samples from this distribution to pick the next token.

📐 Next-Token Probability

Given context: "The capital of France is" Logits (raw scores): Paris→8.5, Lyon→5.2, London→4.1, Berlin→3.8, ... After softmax: Paris→0.89, Lyon→0.06, London→0.03, Berlin→0.02, ... Claude doesn't always pick "Paris" (0.89 probability). With temperature=1.0, it samples: picks Paris 89% of the time, Lyon 6% of the time, etc. Why not always pick the max (greedy)? → Deterministic output = boring, repetitive text → Sampling = diverse, creative text

6.5 Temperature — The Creativity Dial

Temperature is the most important sampling parameter. It controls how "sharp" or "flat" the probability distribution is before sampling — effectively controlling the randomness and creativity of outputs.

📐 Temperature Formula

Modified probability with temperature T: p_i = exp(logit_i / T) / Σ_j exp(logit_j / T) T → 0: Distribution peaks sharply at highest-probability token (near-deterministic, always picks most likely token) T = 1: Standard softmax — no modification T → ∞: All tokens become equally likely (pure random sampling) Practical range for Claude API: 0.0 to 1.0 (Anthropic caps at 1.0)
TemperatureBehaviorBest ForExample Output
0.0Deterministic — always picks most probable tokenFactual Q&A, code, structured data"The capital of France is Paris."
0.3Low randomness — mostly predictable but slight variationTechnical writing, summarizationConsistent, accurate, slightly varied
0.7Moderate creativity — good balanceGeneral chat, analysisNatural, varied but coherent
1.0High creativity — more surprising choicesCreative writing, brainstormingImaginative, sometimes unexpected

6.6 Top-P (Nucleus) Sampling

Top-p sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020) samples only from the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds p. This adapts to the model's confidence: when the model is certain, the nucleus is small (few tokens); when uncertain, the nucleus is larger.

📐 Top-P Sampling

Given probability distribution: Paris→0.89, Lyon→0.06, London→0.03, Berlin→0.02 With top_p = 0.95: Sort by probability: Paris(0.89), Lyon(0.06), London(0.03), ... Cumulative sum: 0.89, 0.95, 0.98, ... Nucleus (≥0.95): {Paris, Lyon} ← only sample from these two Renormalize and sample from {Paris, Lyon} With top_p = 0.99: Nucleus: {Paris, Lyon, London, Berlin} ← more diversity With top_p = 1.0: Nucleus: entire vocabulary ← standard sampling

6.7 Top-K Sampling & Best Practices

Top-k sampling simply keeps only the k highest-probability tokens and samples from them. Unlike top-p, the cutoff is a fixed count rather than adaptive.

ParameterRangeEffectRecommended Setting
temperature0.0–1.0Controls overall randomness0.0 for facts/code; 0.7–1.0 for creative
top_p0.0–1.0Nucleus size (adaptive cutoff)0.95 default; lower for more focus
max_tokens1–200,000Maximum output lengthSet to expected output length + buffer
stop_sequenceslist of stringsHalt generation at specific textUse for structured output (e.g., ["```"])

🔑 Parameter Combinations for Common Tasks

  • Factual Q&A / RAG: temperature=0.0, top_p=1.0 → maximally deterministic
  • Code generation: temperature=0.0–0.2 → correctness over creativity
  • Chat / conversation: temperature=0.7, top_p=0.95 → natural, varied
  • Creative writing: temperature=0.9–1.0, top_p=0.95 → imaginative
  • Brainstorming: temperature=1.0, top_p=0.99 → maximum diversity

7.4 Multi-turn Conversations

Claude is stateless — it has no memory between API calls. To have a conversation, you must include the entire conversation history in each request. Build a history list and append each turn.

multi_turn.py — Conversation with History
import anthropic
client = anthropic.Anthropic()

conversation_history = []  # Stores all turns

def chat(user_message):
    # Add user turn to history
    conversation_history.append({
        "role": "user",
        "content": user_message
    })

    # Send entire history each time
    response = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-3-5-sonnet-20241022",
        max_tokens=512,
        system="You are a helpful assistant.",
        messages=conversation_history  # full history!
    )

    assistant_reply = response.content[0].text

    # Add Claude's reply to history for next turn
    conversation_history.append({
        "role": "assistant",
        "content": assistant_reply
    })
    return assistant_reply

# Example conversation
print(chat("My name is Sansar. I am a professor."))
print(chat("What is my name?"))  # Claude remembers: "Your name is Sansar"
print(chat("What do I do for work?"))  # "You are a professor"

7.6 Error Handling

API calls can fail for many reasons. Always wrap API calls in try/except blocks for production code.

error_handling.py — Production-Ready
import anthropic
import time

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

def call_claude_with_retry(messages, max_retries=3):
    for attempt in range(max_retries):
        try:
            response = client.messages.create(
                model="claude-3-5-sonnet-20241022",
                max_tokens=512,
                messages=messages
            )
            return response.content[0].text

        except anthropic.RateLimitError:
            # Too many requests — wait and retry
            wait = 2 ** attempt  # exponential backoff: 1s, 2s, 4s
            print(f"Rate limited. Waiting {wait}s...")
            time.sleep(wait)

        except anthropic.APIConnectionError:
            # Network issue
            print("Connection error. Check your internet.")
            raise

        except anthropic.AuthenticationError:
            # Bad API key
            print("Invalid API key. Check ANTHROPIC_API_KEY.")
            raise

        except anthropic.BadRequestError as e:
            # Invalid request (e.g., content policy, bad params)
            print(f"Bad request: {e}")
            raise

    raise Exception("Max retries exceeded")
